The description of EM wave polarization

朱宝成1

For a EM wave, we denote the direction of E field as the direction of polarization, the most general form for a EM wave propagating in space is \begin{align} \begin{split} \vec{E}(t)=&\hat{h}E_h+\hat{v}E_v\\ =&\hat{h}e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)+\hat{v}e_vcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v) \label{1} \end{split} \end{align} where $\hat{k},\hat{h},\hat{v}$ form a triad, and $e_h,e_v$ are assumed to be positive.
(As a rule of thumb, we see the polarization state should be determined by $e_h/e_v$ and $\psi_v-\psi_h$, also, if we keep the E field normalized, then it could be described by 2 parameters, which form the face of a sphere, this is called Poincaré Sphere. Of cause, we could using exponent to denote E field as \begin{align} \begin{split} \vec{E}(t)=&\hat{h}e_he^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)}+\hat{v}e_ve^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)} \\ =&(\hat{h}e_h+\hat{v}e_ve^{i\psi})e^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)} \end{split} \end{align} where $\psi=\psi_v-\psi_h$ is the phase different between $E_h$ and $E_v$. )
For example:
  1. $\psi=2m\pi$,($m=0,1,2,\cdots$)
    \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=(\hat{h}e_h+\hat{v}e_v)e^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)} \end{align} is linear polarized in first and third quadrant.
  2. $\psi=(2m+1)\pi$,($m=0,1,2,\cdots$)
    \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=(\hat{h}e_h-\hat{v}e_v)e^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)} \end{align} is linear polarized in second and fourth quadrant.
  3. $\psi=\pm\pi/2$ and $e_h=e_v=e_0$
    \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=e_0(\hat{h}\pm i\hat{v})e^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)} \end{align} represents left/right circular polarization respectively.
  4. $\psi=\pm\pi/2$ and $e_h\neq e_v$
    \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=(\hat{h}e_h\pm i\hat{v}e_v)e^{i(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)} \end{align} represents left/right elliptical polarization respectively, the major and minor axis are along $\hat{h}$ and $\hat{v}$ direction. We have \begin{align} \begin{split} \vec{E}(t)=&\hat{h}e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)\mp\hat{v}e_vsin(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)\\ =&\hat{h}E_h+\hat{v}E_v \end{split} \end{align} \begin{align} \Rightarrow (\frac{E_h}{e_h})^2+(\frac{E_v}{e_v})^2=1 \end{align} when $e_h>e_v$, $e_h$ is the major axis, $e_v$ is the minor axis.
For the general elliptical polarized wave, from Eq.(\ref{1}) \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=&\hat{h}E_h+\hat{v}E_v \nonumber\\ =&\hat{h}e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)+\hat{v}e_vcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v) \end{align} The polarization ellipse shows below,
Figure 1: Elliptical Polarization
Here, $e_1$ is the major axis, $e_2$ is the minor axis, and we define \begin{align} tan\beta=\frac{e_2}{e_1} \quad\quad\quad \begin{array}{ll} 0\leq\beta\leq\pi/4, & \hbox{left-hand polarization;} \\ -\pi/4\leq\beta\leq 0, & \hbox{right-hand polarization.} \end{array} \end{align} From Eq.(\ref{1}), we have $$ \frac{E_h}{e_h}=cos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h) $$$$ \frac{E_v}{e_v}=cos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v) $$ that is \begin{align} \frac{E_v}{e_v}=\frac{E_h}{e_h}cos\psi-\sqrt{1-(\frac{E_h}{e_h})^2}sin\psi \nonumber \\ \Rightarrow (\frac{E_h}{e_h})^2+(\frac{E_v}{e_v})^2-2\frac{E_h}{e_h}\frac{E_v}{e_v}cos\psi=sin^2\psi \end{align} (Or notice that \begin{align} \frac{E_h}{e_h}sin\psi_v-\frac{E_v}{e_v}sin\psi_h=cos(kx-\omega t)sin\psi \\ \frac{E_h}{e_h}cos\psi_v-\frac{E_v}{e_v}cos\psi_h=sin(kx-\omega t)sin\psi \end{align} Eliminating the time dependence $t$, we have \begin{align} (\frac{E_h}{e_h})^2+(\frac{E_v}{e_v})^2-2\frac{E_h}{e_h}\frac{E_v}{e_v}cos\psi=sin^2\psi \end{align} )
We can write the E field in major/minor coordinate system, first we have the unit vector along major and minor axis are \begin{align} \begin{split} \hat{1}=&cos\alpha\hat{h}+sin\alpha\hat{v} \\ \hat{2}=&-sin\alpha\hat{h}+cos\alpha\hat{v} \label{14} \end{split} \end{align} where $\alpha\in[0,\pi)$, that is \begin{align} \begin{split} \hat{h}=&cos\alpha\hat{1}-sin\alpha\hat{2} \\ \hat{v}=&sin\alpha\hat{1}+cos\alpha\hat{2} \end{split} \end{align} The general E field can also be written as \begin{align} \vec{E}(t)=\hat{1}e_1cos(kx-\omega t+\psi_0)-\hat{2}e_2sin(kx-\omega t+\psi_0) \label{15} \end{align} From Eq.(\ref{1}), we also get \begin{align} \begin{split} \vec{E}(t)=&\hat{1}e_1cos(kx-\omega t+\psi_0)-\hat{2}e_2sin(kx-\omega t+\psi_0)\\ =&\hat{h}e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)+\hat{v}e_vcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)\\ =&\hat{1}(e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)cos\alpha+e_vcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)sin\alpha)\\ +&\hat{2}(-e_hcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_h)sin\alpha+e_vcos(kx-\omega t+\psi_v)cos\alpha) \end{split} \end{align} We get \begin{align} e_1cos\psi_0=e_hcos\psi_hcos\alpha+e_vcos\psi_vsin\alpha \label{17a}\\ e_1sin\psi_0=e_hsin\psi_hcos\alpha+e_vsin\psi_vsin\alpha \label{17b}\\ e_2cos\psi_0=-e_hsin\psi_hsin\alpha+e_vsin\psi_vcos\alpha \label{17c}\\ e_2sin\psi_0=e_hcos\psi_hsin\alpha-e_vcos\psi_vcos\alpha \label{17d} \end{align} $(Eq.(\ref{17a}))^2+(Eq.(\ref{17b}))^2$, we get \begin{align} e_1^2=e_h^2cos^2\alpha+e_v^2sin^2\alpha+e_he_vsin2\alpha cos\psi \label{18} \end{align} $(Eq.(\ref{17c}))^2+(Eq.(\ref{17d}))^2$, we get \begin{align} e_2^2=e_1^2tan^2\beta=e_h^2sin^2\alpha+e_v^2cos^2\alpha-e_he_vsin2\alpha cos\psi \label{19} \end{align} Note that $e_1^2+e_2^2=e_h^2+e_v^2$.
$Eq.(\ref{17a})\cdot Eq.(\ref{17c})+Eq.(\ref{17b})\cdot Eq.(\ref{17d})$, we get \begin{align} e_1^2tan\beta=e_he_vsin\psi \label{20} \end{align} $Eq.(\ref{17a})\cdot Eq.(\ref{17d})-Eq.(\ref{17b})\cdot Eq.(\ref{17c})$, we get \begin{align} 2e_he_vcos\psi=(e_h^2-e_v^2)tan2\alpha \label{21} \end{align}
Now we define four Stokes parameters as \begin{align} S_0=&\frac{1}{\eta}(e_h^2+e_v^2) \label{22a}\\ S_1=&\frac{1}{\eta}(e_h^2-e_v^2) \label{22b}\\ S_2=&\frac{2}{\eta}e_he_vcos\psi \label{22c}\\ S_3=&\frac{2}{\eta}e_he_vsin\psi \label{22d} \end{align} $S_0$ is the total intensity of the wave, $S_1$ is large(in absolute value) if it has a preference for linear polarization along $x$ or $y$, $S_2$ is large when polarization is along $\pm 45^o$,$S_3$ is large when it is circular polarized. Notice that $S_0^2=S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2$, $\eta$ can set to be $e_h^2+e_v^2$ to make $S_0$ normalized.
Adding Eq.(\ref{18}) and Eq.(\ref{19}), we have \begin{align} \begin{split} &e_1^2(1+tan^2\beta)=\eta S_0 \\ &\Rightarrow e_1^2=\eta S_0 cos^2\beta \label{23} \end{split} \end{align} Subtracting Eq.(\ref{19}) from Eq.(\ref{18}) and making use of Eq.(\ref{21}) and Eq.(\ref{23}), we have \begin{align} S_1=S_0cos2\beta cos2\alpha \label{24} \end{align} From Eq.(\ref{22c}),Eq.(\ref{21}),Eq.(\ref{24}), we have \begin{align} S_2=S_0cos2\beta sin2\alpha \label{25} \end{align} From Eq.(\ref{22d}),Eq.(\ref{20}),Eq.(\ref{23}), we have \begin{align} S_3=S_0sin2\beta \label{26} \end{align} Define $\theta=\pi/2-2\beta,\phi=2\alpha$, we get \begin{align} S_1=&S_0sin\theta cos\phi \\ S_2=&S_0sin\theta sin\phi \\ S_3=&S_0cos\theta \end{align} $\theta,\phi$ defines the Poincaré Sphere(Fig.2), with $\theta\in[0,\pi/2)$ is right-hand polarization and $\theta\in(\pi/2,\pi]$ is left-hand polarization. We see that $\beta$ is the departure from linear polarization and $\alpha$ is the ratio of $E_h$ and $E_v$ component.
Figure 2: Poincaré Sphere
$\alpha,\beta$ related to $\psi,e_h,e_v$ through \begin{align} tan\beta=&\frac{e_2}{e_1},\\ tan2\alpha=&\frac{2e_he_vcos\psi}{e_h^2-e_v^2}. \end{align} The polarization vector in Poincaré Sphere $(\theta,\phi)$ is \begin{align} e^{i\gamma}\left( \begin{array}{c} cos\frac{\theta}{2} \\ sin\frac{\theta}{2}e^{i\phi} \\ \end{array} \right). \end{align} It corresponding to the eigenstate \begin{align} |\Psi\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( \begin{array}{c} E_h-iE_v \\ E_h+iE_v \\ \end{array} \right)=\left( \begin{array}{c} \Psi_l \\ \Psi_r\\ \end{array} \right) \end{align} of the polarization matrix \begin{align} \textbf{H}(\textbf{r})=\textbf{r}\cdot\sigma=\frac{1}{2}\left( \begin{array}{cc} z & x-iy \\ x+iy & -z \\ \end{array} \right)=\frac{1}{2}\left( \begin{array}{cc} cos\theta & sin\theta e^{-i\phi} \\ sin\theta e^{i\phi} & -cos\theta \\ \end{array} \right). \end{align} where $\sigma$ is the vector of Pauli spin matrix and $\textbf{r}=(x,y,z)$ is a unit vector with polar angles $\theta$ and $\phi$.
In literature, we also have Jones vector, which is defined by \begin{align} |\Psi'\rangle=\left( \begin{array}{c} E_h \\ E_v \\ \end{array} \right)=e^{i\delta}\left( \begin{array}{c} cos\alpha cos\beta-isin\alpha sin\beta \\ sin\alpha cos\beta+icos\alpha sin\beta \\ \end{array}\right), \label{30} \end{align} where $\alpha,\beta$ is what we defined before and $\delta=\gamma+\alpha$.
(Prove: From Eq.(\ref{15}),we can get \begin{align} E_1=&e_1cos\psi_0\\ E_2=&-e_1tan\beta sin\psi_0=ie_1tan\beta cos\psi_0=itan\beta E_1 \end{align} we have omitted the phase factor $kx-\omega t$, from Eq.(\ref{14}), we get \begin{align} E_h=cos\alpha E_1-sin\alpha E_2\\ E_v=sin\alpha E_1+cos\alpha E_2 \end{align} \begin{align} \Rightarrow E_h=&E_1(cos\alpha-isin\alpha tan\beta)=E_1'(cos\alpha cos\beta-isin\alpha sin\beta)\\ E_v=&E_1(sin\alpha+icos\alpha tan\beta)=E_1'(sin\alpha cos\beta+icos\alpha sin\beta) \end{align} which is just Eq.(\ref{30}).)
So we can see that Jones vector is convenient when dealing with linear polarization and polarization transformation, while Poincaré vector is convenient for circular polarized beam.
For partial polarization beam, Stokes parameters show their natural merit. Generalize the definition: \begin{align} S_0=&\frac{1}{\eta}(<E_h^2>+<E_v^2>) \\ S_1=&\frac{1}{\eta}(<E_h^2>-<E_v^2>)=S_0<cos2\beta cos2\alpha> \\ S_2=&\frac{2}{\eta}<E_hE_vcos\psi>=\frac{2}{\eta}<Re(E_h^*E_v>=S_0<cos2\beta sin2\alpha> \\ S_3=&\frac{2}{\eta}<E_hE_vsin\psi>= \frac{2}{\eta}<Re(-iE_h^*E_v>=S_0<sin2\beta> \end{align} For completely unpolarized waves, $E_h$ and $E_v$ are uncorrelated, we have $S_1=S_2=S_3=0$, so for partial polarized waves, we have $S_0^2>S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2$. We can split the partial polarized beam into two components, \begin{align} \vec{S}=\vec{S}_{un}+\vec{S}_p \end{align} $\vec{S_{un}}$represents unpolarized part and $\vec{S_p}$ the totally polarized part with \begin{align} \vec{S}=&(S_0,S_1,S_2,S_3)\\ \vec{S}_{un}=&([S_0-\sqrt{S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2}],0,0,0)\\ \vec{S}_p=&(\sqrt{S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2},S_1,S_2,S_3) \end{align} and we can define the degree of polarization $\mathscr{P}$ as \begin{align} \mathscr{P}=\frac{\sqrt{S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2}}{S_0} \end{align} then $\vec{S}=S_0(1,\mathscr{P}cos2\beta cos2\alpha,\mathscr{P}cos2\beta sin2\alpha,\mathscr{P}sin2\beta)$, which is in the Poincar\'{e} Sphere.
